Growth & Employment

Real GDP Growth

+0.5% ↓ from 4.4%

Annualized quarter-over-quarter % change · Quarterly

Definition

Real GDP is the total value of everything the US economy produces — goods and services — adjusted for inflation so you can compare it fairly over time. It's the broadest scoreboard for how fast (or slow) the economy is growing.

When this goes up: the economy is expanding, businesses are producing more, and jobs are usually plentiful. When this goes down: the economy is shrinking — two consecutive quarters of negative growth is the classic definition of a recession.

Unemployment Rate

4.3% → Steady

U-3 unemployment rate · Monthly, seasonally adjusted

Definition

The unemployment rate is the percentage of people who want a job but can't find one, counting only those who are actively looking. It's the most widely cited snapshot of US labor market health.

When this goes up: more people are out of work — a signal of economic stress that often prompts the Fed to cut rates. When this goes down: the job market is tight, wages tend to rise, and the economy is generally in good shape.

Inflation

Consumer Price Index

3.3% ↑ from 2.4%

Headline CPI, all items · Year-over-year % change · Monthly

Definition

The CPI tracks how much prices have changed over the past year for everyday purchases — groceries, gas, housing, healthcare, and more. It's the most familiar measure of inflation and the one that hits household budgets most directly.

When this goes up: your money doesn't go as far — prices are rising faster, which can squeeze budgets and push the Fed to raise rates. When this goes down: inflation is cooling, giving consumers more purchasing power and the Fed room to cut rates.

Core CPI

2.6% ↓ Easing trend

CPI ex. food & energy · Year-over-year % change · Monthly

Definition

Core CPI is the regular CPI with food and energy prices removed — since gas and grocery prices swing wildly with seasons and global events, stripping them out reveals the slower-moving, underlying inflation trend.

When this goes up: stubborn underlying price pressures are building, making the Fed more likely to hold rates high. When this goes down: the structural inflation trend is cooling, giving the Fed confidence to consider cutting rates.

PCE Price Index

3.5% ↑ from 2.7%

Personal Consumption Expenditures · YoY % change · Monthly (through Feb 2026)

Definition

The PCE is the Federal Reserve's preferred inflation measure. It's similar to CPI but broader — it accounts for the fact that people switch to cheaper products when prices rise, and it includes healthcare costs paid by employers on workers' behalf.

When this goes up: inflation is running hotter than the Fed's 2% target, making rate cuts less likely. When this goes down: inflation is approaching the target, clearing the path for the Fed to lower borrowing costs.

Definition

Core PCE strips food and energy out of the PCE to show the Fed the inflation trend it can most directly influence through interest rate decisions. It is the single number the Fed watches most closely when deciding whether to raise, hold, or cut rates.

When this goes up: the Fed is likely to hold or raise rates to keep inflation from becoming entrenched. When this falls toward 2%: the Fed's job is getting done and rate cuts become increasingly plausible.

Monetary Policy & Rates

Federal Funds Rate

3.63% → On hold

Target rate midpoint (3.50–3.75% range) · Monthly

Definition

The federal funds rate is the interest rate the Federal Reserve sets for banks lending to each other overnight. It's the main dial the Fed turns to speed up or slow down the economy, since it ripples into mortgage, auto loan, and credit card rates.

When this goes up: borrowing costs rise across the board, cooling spending and investment to fight inflation. When this goes down: loans get cheaper, encouraging spending and business investment to stimulate a sluggish economy.

10-Year Treasury Yield

US 10-Year Constant Maturity · % yield · Monthly avg

Definition

The 10-year Treasury yield is the interest rate the US government pays to borrow money for 10 years. It's the global benchmark for long-term interest rates — it directly shapes mortgage rates, corporate borrowing costs, and how investors value stocks.

When this goes up: long-term borrowing gets more expensive, which weighs on home buyers, companies taking on debt, and stock prices. When this goes down: financing costs ease, making it cheaper to get a mortgage, grow a business, or fund government spending.

ISM Manufacturing PMI

52.7 Expanding ↑

Purchasing Managers' Index · 50 = expansion threshold · Monthly

Definition

The ISM Manufacturing PMI is a monthly survey asking factory purchasing managers whether business conditions — orders, production, hiring — got better or worse. A score above 50 means manufacturing is expanding; below 50 means it's contracting. It's one of the first economic indicators released each month.

When this goes above 50: factories are ramping up, pointing to more jobs and economic momentum. When this falls below 50: manufacturing is shrinking, which is often an early warning sign of broader economic weakness ahead.

Definition

The ISM Services PMI is the same survey concept as the Manufacturing PMI, but for service industries — restaurants, healthcare, finance, retail — which collectively make up about 80% of the US economy. Above 50 means growth; below 50 means contraction.

Housing Starts

1.487M ↑ +7.2% MoM

New residential construction starts · SAAR millions · Monthly (through Jan 2026)

Definition

Housing starts count how many new homes broke ground in a given month, scaled to an annual rate. Because building a home requires labor, materials, and affordable financing, it's a useful barometer for the health of the construction industry and interest-rate-sensitive sectors of the economy.

When this goes up: builders are confident, mortgage rates are manageable, and the housing market is active — a positive signal for growth. When this goes down: high mortgage rates or weak demand are discouraging new construction, which can signal a cooling economy and eventually worsen the housing shortage.

Trade Balance

-$57B ↑ Narrowed Feb

Goods & services trade balance · $ billions · Monthly (through Feb 2026)

Definition

The trade balance is simply exports minus imports — a positive number means the US sells more abroad than it buys; a negative number (a deficit) means the US buys more from other countries than it sells to them.

When the deficit narrows: the US is exporting more or importing less, which adds directly to GDP. When the deficit widens: imports are surging — sometimes a sign of strong domestic demand, but it subtracts from GDP and can reflect reliance on foreign goods.

University of Michigan Consumer Sentiment

UMich Consumer Sentiment Index (1966=100) · Monthly

Definition

This index measures how optimistic or pessimistic Americans feel about their finances and the economy, based on a monthly survey. Since consumer spending drives most of the US economy, sentiment is watched as a leading signal for whether people are likely to open or close their wallets.

When this goes up: people feel more financially secure and are likely to spend, supporting economic growth. When this goes down: worry is rising — about jobs, prices, or uncertainty — and spending may slow before the hard economic data even catches up.
